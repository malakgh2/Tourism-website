Tabarka

Parc Ichkeul





Tabarka (Arabic: طبرقة‎ About this soundṬbarqa) is a coastal town located in north-western Tunisia, close to the border with Algeria. Tabarka's history is a mosaic of Berber, Punic, Hellenistic, Roman, Arabic, Genoese and Turkish culture. The town is dominated by an offshore rock on which there remains a Genoese castle. Nationalist leader Habib Bourguiba, later president of post-independence Tunisia, was exiled on Tabarka by the French colonial authorities in 1952.Tourist attractions include coral fishing, the Coralis Festival of underwater photography, and its annual jazz festival

Fort Guénois

the Genoese fort witnessed to the presence of the Italians for two centuries, from 1542 to 1742. The building preserves the memory of the "Tabarquins". It should be remembered that the Tabarquins are the inhabitants of the island of Tabarka, the demographic fabric got constituted in the thirteenth century thanks to Italian coral fishermen and traders. According to a legend, the noble Genoese family of Guenois, they have received the island following an agreement signed, in 1542, with Alfonso de Cardona, representative of the emperor Charles V. later, the family founded the village with two ports and an arsenal for coralines the Genoese fort was classified as a historical monument in 1920.
